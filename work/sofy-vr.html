Joey Qiang

Sofy VR

  • UX design
  • July, 2016
  • SOFY

I participated in the virtual reality project of SOFY as an intern UX designer at Mobile Now Group (MNG). SOFY VR game is a mobile shooter game. Users need to download SOFY app on their smart-phone first, and after launching the game, they need to wear VR glasses to play the game by moving the crosshair on the enemies.

It's an interesting and challenging experience for me because it’s the first time that I had the responsibility to directly negotiate with the client. Not only did I collaborate with my colleagues to finish my design work, but also I had to present our team’s ideas to the client to move the project forward.

Context

We needed to get more done within a short time

MNG worked as an agency to help SOFY design a mobile VR game. Due to SOFY’s urgent schedule, we needed to finish game design in the first week so the VR game developer company, called Ant Hive, could develop the game demo in advance, saving time for testing.

Later, we had to hand over remaining deliverables to them, including wireframes and user interfaces. Therefore, as an interaction designer, I had to push myself and my teammates to work in a rapidly iterated pace for the purpose of meeting the deadline.

After the game was finished, SOFY would advertise the game by binding free VR glasses to its products, attracting users to buy their products.

Our goal is to design an interesting and easy-play VR shooting game for teen girls with the purpose of increasing the recognition of SOFY.

Game concept

Shooting the foods which are considered bad for girls experiencing menstruation

Because we wanted to relate the game to SOFY’s sanitary pads, finally we determined to design the game’s characters as foods that girls cannot eat when experiencing menstruation. Besides, because our target audience was girls 18-24 years old, we chose cute cartoons as the game style.

Below were challenges we faced:

  1. Aiming to shoot is the only interaction mode we can use, while balancing the appeal and complexity of the game.
  2. Collaborating well with the developer company to implement design, while meeting the urgent deadline.

To make the game easy to play, users need to do only one thing — moving the crosshair to the enemy they want to shoot. And there is no time limit during the game. Users focus only on shooting enemies as fast as possible, since we wanted users to be totally absorbed in the game.

Also, to make the game interesting to play, we designed three obstacles to make users experience new things in different levels.

  • Spawn distance
    The higher the level, the greater distance where enemies can spawn
  • Teleport
    Monsters have 30% chance to teleport to the position with a same X coordinate when their HP reduces to 50%
  • Shield
    A 2 second shield will be randomly activated on certain monsters that are close to the crosshair

Once a level is done, the game shows users the total points collected based on the number of eliminated monsters. When users reach a certain amount of points, the game will unlock the next level with new monsters and rules. The calculation will be based on points collected for monsters eliminated and points deducted for monsters that touched the player. The monsters will spawn in 3 waves in every level.

And there are three levels in total with different difficulty. The game consists of 5 monsters and 2 special items, one being the rabbit, which eliminates all monsters on your screen, and the other being the pad, which players need to avoid shooting.

Process

Every design should have a reason behind it

After our team finished the game concept design, we delivered our ideas to Ant Hive. Their game designers were responsible for all visual design of the VR game, such as moving, hitting, and killing status of each monster. Then, I began to work on the pre-game, game-in-progress, and post-game workflow design.

Below are requirements for my wireframe:

  1. The game feature should be easily noticed and the game can be quickly started.
  2. Creating a friendly experience for users to transfer from non-VR part to VR part
  3. Adding a SOFY E-Commerce entry to boost product sales

To make the game feature outstanding, I added the new game entry button to the top of the existing app and marked a “NEW” text to make it outstanding. After users click the button, they can get into the game. And during the loading time, I added a loading image to keep their interest.

For the home page, I placed the side navigation bar to leave enough space for the main content. The navigation bar contains 3 tabs：selecting levels, points redemption, and E-commerce. The default active tab is selecting levels. For selecting levels panel, it shows three levels which contain level’s title, level’s introduction, and functional buttons, such as tutorial button and start button. If the level is locked, I put a lock icon on the top of the panel.

After users click the tutorial button, they go to the tutorial page, which has two parts —about friend and about enemy. For each part, users can click the arrow to switch characters. Also, users have the option of jumping tutorial; And I provided start button on the last panel. For the tutorial panel, at first I overlooked several problems, which I will explain later.

For the game flow, after users click the start button, the interface notifies users to put their smartphone into VR glasses to play the game.

Because there is a time-delay when users wear VR glasses, the game won’t start until users aim at the start button for 1 second. After one level finishes, users go to the game-over page which presents all information from this round. And I also provided a progress bar to tell users how many points they still need to get to unlock next level.

On the redemption page, users can view their current points and redemption history. But due to the imitation of business issues, they can only redeem all points at once. I didn’t think it was a good marketing idea. I gave feedback to my director that I wanted users can directly user coupons to redeem SOFY’s products, which obviously produce more motivation for users to play the game and attract more people to use SOFY products. However, my director told me we could do nothing about this part.

Then I realized that design is not only about designing a product, but also relates to balancing business goals and design goals.

Modification

Rapid iteration helps the team understand what the client really wants

After I collaborated with UI designer in finishing the first high-fidelity version, I needed to prove my design towards clients because the PM cannot speak Chinese with Chinese clients. I was confident to deliver all my ideas to clients but then I realized that I missed important details after clients pointed out their concerns.

Here are concerns clients had:

  1. The start game button was not conspicuous to see and button size should be bigger considering the VR scenario.
  2. The interaction mode of selecting levels was inconsistent. At the beginning users select levels with their fingers, but in the end users select levels with their eyes.
  3. The tutorial pages were tedious and we should let users browse them as quickly as possible.
  4. The ending page lacked a sense of reward, which could motivate users to play longer.

After much consideration, I overthrew my previous design and came up with better ideas to solve these concerns. First, I changed the home page layout to highlight the START button and enlarged buttons' size to make them easier to be controlled. Then I changed the way to select levels from manual control to eye control, making it easier for users to switch levels without taking off VR glasses.

Also, I changed the browsing gesture of tutorial page from left & right clicking to up & down scrolling, which greatly expedited users' browsing speed.

Last but not least, I added extra details for the ending page with a pad and a rabbit fading in together, to give users a sense of reward. Then characters fade out and level selection fades in.

After we put up the new design detailing all these changes, clients happily accepted our final design.

Reflection

Put yourself into users' shoes when you design

I realized that I made many design flaws because I designed the product mostly from my personal experience. I missed many edge cases from a real user’s point of view at the very beginning. I should have thought the whole game flow from an amateur gamer’s point of view. It’s necessary for designers to have the ability to switch to users’ thinking model.

For example, was the button a call to action? CTA buttons must be adequately sized and a minimum of 44 points is usually sufficient. Was the button big enough to be controlled by the eyes? Clicking the button by using eyes is more difficult than by fingers. Considering the usage scenarios is very important. What is the primary goal of users? After you decide the primary goal, try your best to make it stand out.

Also, clients are your users too. Design a smart way to collaborate with them and make them happy with your design.
